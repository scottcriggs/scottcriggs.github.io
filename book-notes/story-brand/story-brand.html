Building a StoryBrand: Notes on Marketing

Summary: The StoryBrand Framework

The StoryBrand Framework involves 7 key elements:

  1. A Character: The customer who wants something.
  2. Has a Problem: Something is making them struggle.
  3. And Meets a Guide: Your brand that has the solution.
  4. Who Gives Them a Plan: A clear path to overcome the problem.
  5. And Calls Them to Action: Telling the customer what to do next.
  6. That Helps Them Avoid Failure: Showing the negative consequences of inaction.
  7. And Ends in Success: Painting a picture of the positive outcome.

Chapter 4: Building a Character

  • Customer as the Hero: The customer is the hero, not your brand!
  • Stories Start with Desire: Stories start with the hero wanting something.
    • Define what our hero wants?
      • Hard Tech Executive / CTO:
        • Decrease the costs of their R&D for realizing their next-generation technology.
        • To see their vision for how this next-gen tech is transformative or additive to the world come to realization.
      • User:
        • Job efficiency (easier way to do their tasks). An easier way to accomplish job responsibilities.
        • Career growth.
        • Scientific curiosity.
    • Define what is our hero's ambition?
      • Hard Tech Executive / CTO:
        • The person responsible for unlocking this new world.
        • To be seen as a leader or expert in that domain.
      • User:
        • Wants to be the person who realizes the executive's vision. They are the person who does the work to achieve the goal (i.e., the grad student doing the experiment and getting the publication).
        • Stand out, without rocking the boat for career security and growth.
    • What does our customer desire relevant to their survival?
      • Conserving resources/money
      • Conserving time
      • Building social networks
      • Gaining status
      • To have a sense of meaning, to be part of something greater
  • Identifying Customer Desires Opens a Story Gap:
    • Story gap: The inherent tension that exists in any good story between:
      • Where the character is now: Their current situation, which usually involves some kind of problem or dissatisfaction.
      • Where the character wants to be: Their desired outcome or goal.
  • Simplify the Message: Pair down desire to a single focus.
  • TODO: What is the story question for your customer?
    • To identify the story question for my customer, I need to consider their desires and challenges when interacting with me.
  • Clear Messaging: The goal is that every customer knows where we want to take them.
  • Clarify Our Message: Clarify our message so customers listen.
  • Discovery Call Questions: Use these questions to discover the answers needed to build a character (i.e., your customer as a hero, with tension):
    • Identify desire related to survival by understanding what they want.
    • The goal of the questions is to address the two points related to creating a story gap.
    • First line of questions:
      • Suppose you succeed and hit all of your goals, how is the world better?
      • How does this future world you've helped enable look better?
      • Given you could have worked on any number of important problems, what made you choose this line of work?
      • What's your vision for the better world that is unlocked for your business objectives?
      • Tell me about your vision for this technology. What impact do you hope it will have on the world? (Listen for challenges and frustrations in their answer.)
      • What are the most exciting aspects of this next-gen technology for you personally? (Their answer might reveal their aspirations and what they hope to achieve.)
      • What are your biggest priorities right now in terms of R&D? (This can uncover pain points and areas where they need help.)
    • Second line of questions:
      • If you could wave a magic wand and solve one major challenge related to realizing your next-gen technology, what would it be?
      • What's the biggest obstacle standing between you and achieving your vision for this technology?
      • What keeps you up at night when you think about bringing this next-gen technology to life?

Chapter 5: The Character Has a Problem

  • Focus on Internal Problems: Companies tend to sell solutions to external problems, but customers buy solutions to internal problems.
  • Highlight the Problem: The more we talk about the problems our customers face, the more interested they will be in our brand.
  • Position Your Brand as the Solution: Position your products and services to take out their villain.
    • Villain doesn't have to be a person, but should be personified.
    • Personify customers' problems as the villain.
  • Characteristics of the Villain:
    • Should be a root source of conflicts.
    • Should be relatable.
    • Should be singular.
    • They should be real.
  • Defining the Villain:
    • What is the chief source of conflict that our products and services defeat?
    • 3 Levels of Conflict from a Villain:
      • External problems: The physical/actual thing we solve.
      • Internal problems: What frustration do our products resolve?
        • Customers buy from us because the external problem is frustrating them in some way. If we can identify that frustration, put it into words, and resolve that frustration along with the original external problem, we will be loved by the customer.
      • Philosophical problems: Usually defined as the "ought" and "shouldn't." What is the deeper meaning?
    • Our product should solve all three of these problems.
    • Buying our product is the resolution to these three problems.
  • Use Case 1:
    • Villain:
      • Competitor
      • Complexity of R&D
      • Their runway/resource cost
      • Market defense/penetration
    • External problem:
      • Optimizing process flows.
      • Efficiently navigating complex multi-dimensional spaces.
      • Making data-driven strategic decisions even in situations without a lot of data, where collecting more data is resource intensive.
    • Internal problem:
      • Resource cost of doing R&D. A lot of resources are spent on R&D without always a good understanding of the ROI (or if it will work).
      • Asking themselves, do they even need this (these AI tools)? Customer knows they should be using AI, but they aren't sure what to do. (We allow them to outsource, so they don't have to take on the risk of developing the internal tool. We've solved this problem over 7 years, use us).
    • Philosophical problem:
      • Our brand ensures that critical expertise and insights (e.g., tribal knowledge) aren't lost, creating a living repository of knowledge that fuels continuous learning and innovation.
      • Knowledge isn't lost when an engineer or employee transfers, or leaves, for example.
  • Use Case 2:
    • Villain: Complexity of R&D
    • External problem: I need to find a winning process flow.
    • Internal problem: Pressure to innovate.
    • Philosophical problem: Unable to spend time understanding the problem
  • Internal Questions:
    • What are the literal and metaphorical villains our brand stands against?
    • What are the external problems our brand resolves? Is there one that seems to encompass the widest swath of products?
    • What are the internal frustrations, problems, or doubts our customers are feeling as it relates to our brand? Is there on that stands out as a universal experience for our customers.
    • Is our brand part of a larger more important story? Is there a philosophical wrong our brand stands against?

Chapter 6: The Guide

  • Customer as Hero, Brand as Guide: The customer is the hero; we are the guide!
  • Focus on Customer Success: The story is NOT about us. We should lose sleep over the success of the customer's business, not ours.
  • Characteristics of a Guide:
    • Empathetic: This creates trust.
    • Authoritative: Have authority, wisdom, or knowledge (establishes competence).
  • Show You Care: Make sure your customers know you care! Say it!
  • Demonstrating Authority Without Arrogance:
    • Testimonials: Generally, no more than 3, should be short.
    • Statistics: (e.g., number of satisfied customers, money saved, etc.)
    • Awards
    • Logos: (place logos of companies you've helped)
  • Trust Building: Showing authority without arrogance allows customers to establish trust.
  • First Impressions:
    • Can I trust this person?
    • Can I respect this person?
  • Internal Questions:
    • What are some empathetic statements our brand can make to show we care about their internal problem?
      • "Don't get bogged down in the technical details of AI. Our intuitive platform allows you to seamlessly integrate your knowledge and insights, making AI accessible to everyone, and freeing up your time and resources to focus on what truly matters – your core expertise and innovation."
      • "We understand that the R&D landscape is too vast to try it all. We help you prioritize the viable options to get results faster."
      • "The search for the winning process flow can be exhausting. We've spent years refining our approach to help you prioritize the viable options, get results faster, and accelerate your time to market."
    • What are some ways to demonstrate authority without arrogance?
      • Professional and educational associations
      • Testimonials
      • Statistics (adoption)

Chapter 7: The Guide Gives the Hero a Plan

  • Trust the Plan: Customers need to trust that the guide has a plan.
  • The Plan Provides Clarity: The plan creates clarity or removes a sense of risk.
  • Plan Types:
    • Process Plan: (meant to eliminate confusion)
      • Steps customer needs to take to use our product, OR
      • Steps customer needs to buy our product, OR
      • A combo of both
    • Post-Purchase Plan: (meant to eliminate confusion)
      • A plan that addresses when customers have problems imagining how they might use our product after the purchase from us.
      • Ideally 3-6 steps. If more, break into phases.
    • Agreement Plan:
      • List of agreements to overcome their fear of doing business with you.
      • Act as a way to increase the perceived value of your service or product.
  • Naming the Plan: Give the title of our plan a name that will increase the perceived value of our product/service.
  • Internal Questions:
    • Define the plan or plans to ease the customers' concerns so they will engage your brand?
    • Clarify the message so customers listen.
    • Simple steps a customer would need to take in order to do business with us. (A pre- or post-purchase plan process)
    • What fears do customers have related to our industry?
    • What agreements can we make to alleviate those fears?
    • Do we share unique values with our customers? Can these values be spelled out in an agreement plan?

Chapter 8: Call Customers to Action

  • Challenge to Act: Customers do not take action unless they are challenged to take action.
  • Clarity and Repetition: Key to sales is clarifying the calls to action and repeating them.
  • Passive Communication: Communicating passively signals a lack of belief in your product.
  • Directness: The guide needs to be direct to the hero about what they want them to do.
  • Two Kinds of Calls to Action:
    • Direct Calls to Action: (i.e., something that leads to a sale)
      • Buy now!
      • Call today!
      • Schedule an Appointment!
    • Transitional Calls to Action: (usually entails less risk and usually involves something free)
      • Download a PDF
      • Watch an informative video
  • Both Are Essential: Always have both a direct call to action (goal is to make a sale) and a transitional call to action.
  • Pursue Your Customers: As a brand, it's our job to pursue our customers.
  • Persistence Pays Off: Those that ask again and again receive.
  • Website Implementation:
    • Have an obvious button that lets the customer have a direct call to action (like a buy-now button). Have it in multiple places; don't be afraid of repetition.
    • At the end of every email.
  • Benefits of Transitional Calls to Action:
    • Staking a claim to that territory.
    • Create reciprocity (give freely).
    • Position ourselves as the guide.
  • Ideas for Transitional Calls to Action:
    • Give free information.
    • Testimonials.
    • Samples.
    • Free trials.
    • A way for customers to connect the dots.
  • Free Consultations: (e.g., let us help them find the right AI solutions for their specific needs and requirements, even if it's not us)
  • Clarity Removes Confusion: Having clear calls to action means customers aren't confused on what they have to do to do business with us.
  • Define the Stakes: Define the stakes of what happens if they do or don't work with us.
  • Brainstorm Questions:
    • What are some direct calls to action?
      • Which one do we want to have on all of our marketing material?
    • What transitional call to action can we come up with that:
      • Stakes a claim or territory
      • Create reciprocity
      • Position brands as a guide

Chapter 9: Avoid a Tragic Ending

  • Every human being is trying to avoid a tragic ending.
  • Hero is motivated by two things:
    • To escape something bad
    • Experience something good
  • The "so-what" question every customer is secretly asking: Where is the mayhem? All brands must answer this question.
  • Answer the question, "What is there to lose by not using our product?"
  • Don't fear-monger, but you must bring up the negative stakes and emphasize potential loss.
  • Loss Aversion: A great motivator is Loss Aversion; people are more likely to be dissatisfied by a loss than satisfied by a gain.
  • Follow a Fear Appeal:
    1. Make a customer know they are vulnerable to a threat.
    2. Let the customer know, since they are vulnerable, they should take action to decrease their vulnerability.
    3. Let the customer know about a specific call to action that protects them from the risk.
    4. Challenge people to take this specific action.
  • Fear is the salt in a recipe. We don't need a lot of it, just a pinch.
  • Too much fear and it doesn't work.
  • Ask the question, what are we helping the customer avoid?
  • Brainstorm questions:
    • Write down at least three negative consequences we are helping our customers avoid (e.g., money, health, etc.).
    • Write down an example of fear appeal in the 4-steps.

Chapter 10: And Ends in a Success

  • Never assume people understand how your brand can change their lives; TELL THEM.
  • Always remember people want to be taken somewhere.
    • Where is our brand taking people?
      • E.g., financial security, fun weekend, ...
  • Brands die without a vision.
  • The happy ending should be SPECIFIC and CLEAR.
    • The resolution must clearly be defined so the customer knows what to hope for.
    • We must tell our customers what their lives will be like after they buy our products or they won't have the motivation to buy them.
    • Show customers the end vision we have for their lives.
    • Show people happily engaged with the product (on the website).
  • Have a list of resolutions to customer problems. Brainstorm:
    • What the customer's life will look like externally if their problem is resolved.
    • How will it make them feel (internal problem resolution).
    • Why resolving their problem has made their world a better place to live in (philosophical resolution).
  • Main goal of our product is to have our customers survive and thrive.
  • Three Desires:
    • Winning power and position: The need for status.
      • Offer access
      • Create scarcity
      • Offer a premium (e.g., "Anchor Partner")
      • Offer identity association
    • Union that makes the hero whole: The need for something external to create completeness.
      • Reduce anxiety
      • Reduce workload
      • Reduce time
    • Ultimate self-realization: The need to reach our potential.
      • Inspiration
      • Acceptance
      • Transcendence
  • Closing the Story Loops:
    • Customers close the story loops to their external, internal, and philosophical problems via one or more of the 3 desires.
    • Keep closure of the story loops SIMPLE.
    • Clarify your message so customers listen.
  • Brainstorm:
    • Brainstorm the successful resolution we are helping our customers achieve.
    • What will their lives look like if they use our products and services?

Chapter 11: Brand Transformation

  • Biggest motivator your customer has in making a purchase: The desire to become somebody different.
  • Our brand helps people become a better version of themselves. More wiser, better equipped, at peace...
  • Our brand is helping in our customers' transformation, which is exactly what they want us to do.
  • Our brand should help with the identity transformation of our customers.
  • Heroes are designed to transform.
  • Questions to ask when representing our brand:
    • Who does our customer want to become?
    • What kind of person do they want to be?
    • What is their aspirational identity?
      • Smart brands define an aspiration identity and associate themselves with that identity.
  • Best way to identify an aspirational identity that our customers may be attracted to: How does our customer want to be described by others? How does our customer want their friends to talk about them?
  • Embolden customers with an aspirational identity; encourage their improvements, tackling their challenges as a step to personal strength, and few problems can't be conquered with a little strategy and commitment.
  • Great brands obsess about the transformation of their customers.
  • Heroes struggle with doubt right up until the end. A hero needs the guide to step in and tell them they are different, they are better.
  • Create an Identity Transformation:
    • Who does your customer want to become as it relates to our products and services?
  • Brainstorm:
    • Find the Aspirational Identity of our customer:
      • Who do they want to become?
      • How do they want to be perceived by others?
    • Fill out the "to" and "from" sections to identify an aspirational identity. "From" is just the opposite of whatever aspirations identity you captured in the "to" line.

Chapter 12: Building a Better Website

  • Keep it Simple: Keep it very simple – equivalent to an elevator pitch.
  • Include these five things to see results:
    1. An offer "above the fold":
      • Things you see before you scroll.
      • Customers need to know what's in it for them.
      • Text above the fold should:
        • Offer an aspirational identity.
        • Promise to solve a problem.
      • State exactly what your company does.
    2. An obvious call to action.
    3. Images of success: (smiling, happy people engaged with our brand/product/service that solved their problem)
    4. Bite-sized breakdown of our revenue streams.
    5. Very few words: Less words = more people read it.
  • Further Detail Pages: Each link, or page from the landing page, should break down a new brand script.

Chapter 13: Using a StoryBrand for Internal Direction and Vision

  • Don't Assume Alignment: Just because you know the story doesn't mean your team does.
  • Narrative Void: Misalignment usually happens when part of the StoryBrand brand script is missing. This is a narrative void.
    • Important to get the organization back on mission.
  • "Thought-Mist-Verse": An invisible mixture of beliefs and ideas that drives employee behavior and performance.
  • Executive's Role: The number 1 job of an executive is to remind the stakeholders what the mission story is.
  • Changing Culture Using StoryBrand Mission Story:
    1. Create a BrandScript with the leadership team.
    2. Audit the existing "Thought-Mist-Verse."
    3. Create a custom StoryBrand culture implementation plan.
    4. Optimize internal communications to support the plan.
    5. Install a team to enhance the culture.
  • Unified Team: A StoryBrand culture turns everyone into a sales force.
  • Guide vs. Hero: Leaders WANT to be seen as the hero, but all the things they want actually come by BEING the guide. Guides are loved, listened to, followed, and have loyalty.
  • Alignment for Success: When the story of the customer and the company align with the story of the team it leads to profit and a healthy work environment.

Chapter 14: Five Things to Grow Our Business

  1. Create a 1-liner: Put it everywhere, back of business card, etc.
    • One-liner has components (character, problem, plan, success).
  2. Create a lead generator and collect email addresses.
    • How to get an email list -> offer something:
      • Downloadable guide
      • Online course or webinar
      • Software demos or free trial
      • Free samples
      • Live events
    • A lead generator must do two things:
      • Provide value
      • Establish us as authority in the field
    • Write nurturing email. Formula for writing a nurturing email:
      • Talk about a problem (perhaps in a way that elicits an emotion).
      • Explain a plan to solve that problem.
      • Describe how life can look once the problem is solved.
      • Include a P.S.; often people only read the PS.
  3. Create an automated email drip campaign.
    • Primary concern of the email:
      • Offer something of value
      • Position our company as the guide
      • Create reciprocity
    • About every 3 or 4 emails in a nurturing campaign should have an offer and call-to-action. Offer needs to be a direct product or service. Formula for this type of email:
      • Talk about a problem.
      • Talk about a service or product we offer to solve that problem.
      • Describe what life looks like for the customer once the problem is solved.
      • Call the customer to a direct action, looking to land a sale.
  4. Collect and tell stories of transformation.
    • Use the questions below for developing customer testimonials and video testimonials:
      • What was the problem you were having before you discovered our product/service?
      • What did the frustration feel like while you were trying to solve the problem?
      • What was different about our product/service?
      • Take us to the moment when you realized our product was actually working to solve your problem.
      • Tell us what life looks like now that your problem is solved or being solved.
  5. Create a system that generates referrals.
    • Below is the step-by-step approach to creating an effective referral system:
      • Identify your existing, ideal customers.
      • Have a story about the problem you solve.
      • Offer a reward to referrals (10% commission, affiliate program, etc.).
      • Automate the work.
    • Example Email w/ short educational video:
      • Dear friend, Thanks for doing business with us. A number of our clients have wanted to tell their friends about how we help customers, but they aren't sure how to do so. We've put together a little video that will help your friends solve [x problem] if you have any friends with [x problem] feel free to send it along. We'd be happy to follow up with any of them and we'll be sure to let you know whether we can help. We know you value your relationships so do we. If your friends are experiencing a problem we've helped you solved we'd love to help them too. If there's anything else we can do please let us know. Sincerely [X]
      • PS [X problem] can be frustrating, if you'd rather introduce us to your friend in person, just let us know. We are more than happy to meet with them in their place of business or at our office.
